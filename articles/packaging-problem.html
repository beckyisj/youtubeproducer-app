Your Videos Aren't Underperforming — Your Packaging Is

March 3, 2026

📦Most channels with underwhelming views don't have a content problem. They've got a packaging problem (trust me on this one). I see it all the time as YouTube producer for Ali Abdaal: great ideas, strong delivery, but thumbnails and titles that just don't do the content justice.

Analysis as of March 2026 · Based on 776 long-form YouTube videos across 57 channels.

When I consult with YouTube channels, the first thing I look at is never the content. It's the packaging. Thumbnails, titles, the first 30 seconds. Because the best video in the world gets zero views if no one clicks it.

Let me give you a real example from a channel I audited. I'm keeping it anonymous, but this pattern? I've seen it across dozens of channels.

The channel: strong content, weak numbers

This channel had around 5,000 subscribers. Excellent interview content, genuinely interesting guests, good production quality. Even a decent upload cadence (2–3 videos a month). But most videos were getting under 1,000 views.

The owner thought the problem was the algorithm. Or the niche being too competitive. Or not posting enough. (Spoiler alert: it was none of those things.)

What does a packaging problem look like?

I ran the channel through a full audit. The issues were immediately clear:

  1. Thumbnails were generic interview screenshots. Think: two people in chairs, mid-conversation. No text, no strong expression, no visual hook. Every single thumbnail looked identical. You couldn't tell what any video was about just by scrolling the channel page.
  2. Titles were descriptive, not compelling. They'd use titles like "[Guest Name] on Building a Business." Accurate, sure, but zero curiosity. Compare that to: "He Built a $50M Business From His Garage (Here's How)." Same content, completely different click-through rate.
  3. No pattern or brand consistency. Fonts, colors, and layouts changed randomly from video to video. Returning viewers couldn't recognize the content at a glance.

How to diagnose your own packaging

Try this test: pull up your own channel page and squint. If you can't tell what any video is about from the thumbnails alone, you've got a packaging problem. If your titles only make sense to someone who's already watched the video, you've got a packaging problem. If your videos look like every other channel in your niche, you've got a packaging problem.

Specifically, check these five things:

  1. Can you read the thumbnail at phone size? Seriously, shrink your thumbnail to 320x180 pixels. If the text is unreadable or the expression is invisible, it's not working on mobile — which is where most YouTube viewing happens.
  2. Does the thumbnail create a question? The best ones make you think "what's going on here?" or "I need to know more." If the thumbnail answers the question instead of posing it, that click incentive just vanishes.
  3. Does the title add new information? Your thumbnail and title should work as a team, not just repeat each other. The thumbnail grabs attention, the title adds context that tips the viewer into clicking.
  4. Would a stranger click this? You know your content is good. But the thumbnail needs to convince someone who's never heard of you. Show your thumbnails to someone outside your niche. If they say "Yeah, I'd watch that," then you're probably good.
  5. Do your thumbnails look consistent as a set? Consistent doesn't mean identical. It means a viewer who liked one video can visually recognize another video from your channel in their feed. Think: same font family, similar color palette, recognizable layout.

The fix is faster than you think

Packaging fixes are the highest-ROI changes you can make on YouTube. Unlike content quality (which takes months to improve) or upload frequency (which requires more time), better thumbnails and titles can be implemented on your very next video.

Start with your top-performing video. What made that thumbnail and title work? Now look at your worst performer. What's different there? That gap between them? That's your opportunity.
