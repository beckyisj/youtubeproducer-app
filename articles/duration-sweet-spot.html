How to Find Your Channel's Duration Sweet Spot

March 3, 2026

⏱️Every channel has a duration sweet spot — that ideal video length where views peak. As YouTube producer for Ali Abdaal, I track this obsessively. I've found that using data is the only way to pinpoint yours, no guesswork required.

Analysis as of March 2026 · Based on 776 long-form YouTube videos across 57 channels.

How long should your YouTube videos really be? It’s one of the most common questions creators ask. The internet is full of confident answers: "10 minutes is optimal." "YouTube prefers longer videos." "Keep it under 15 minutes."

They’re all wrong, by the way. The right answer is different for every single channel. Your audience has a specific appetite for your content, and the data will tell you exactly what that is.

What is a duration sweet spot?

Your duration sweet spot is the length bucket where your videos consistently pull in the highest median views. We’re talking median here, not average (which gets easily skewed by outliers) — it’s about the true middle of your channel’s performance.

When I’m analyzing a channel, I break duration into 8 specific buckets:

  • Under 5 minutes
  • 5–10 minutes
  • 10–15 minutes
  • 15–20 minutes
  • 20–30 minutes
  • 30–45 minutes
  • 45–60 minutes
  • 60+ minutes

For each of those buckets, I calculate the median views across all videos in that range (you need at least 3 videos for the data to actually mean something). The bucket that shows the highest median? That’s your sweet spot.

Why does video length affect views?

Video duration impacts views in two main ways:

  1. Watch time signals. YouTube’s algorithm loves total watch time. A 20-minute video where 60% of viewers watch to the end *will* generate more watch time than a 5-minute video with 90% retention. But if that 20-minute video loses everyone by minute 3? It’s actually worse than the shorter one.
  2. Audience expectations. Your subscribers develop a real sense of how long your videos "should" be. If you usually post 15-minute videos and suddenly drop a 60-minute epic, some viewers just won’t click — they don’t have an hour. The reverse is true, too: if your audience expects deep dives and you post a 5-minute surface-level video, they’ll feel shortchanged. (Seriously, it happens.)

How do I find my channel's sweet spot?

Sure, you could do this manually. Sort your videos by view count, then painstakingly look at each duration. But trust me, it’s tedious and prone to mistakes, especially once you have hundreds of videos.

What if I don't have enough videos?

You really need at least 3 videos in a duration bucket for the data to be meaningful. If you only have, say, 20 videos total, some buckets will be empty or just have 1–2 videos. That’s simply not enough to draw any real conclusions.

In that case, look at your niche instead. Audit 3–5 channels similar to yours — think same topic, similar size — and see where their sweet spots tend to cluster. That gives you a solid starting hypothesis to test with your own content.

Common sweet spot patterns by niche

From the channels I’ve audited, I’ve started to notice some rough patterns. Keep in mind, your channel may differ:

  • Money/Finance: 10–20 minutes. Long enough to feel substantive, but still short enough to watch on a lunch break.
  • Self-Improvement: 10–15 minutes. Actionable advice that doesn’t overstay its welcome.
  • Podcasts/Interviews: 30–60+ minutes. The audience *expects* long-form. Going short here often feels like a highlight reel, not a full conversation.
  • Creator/YouTube advice: 10–15 minutes. Creators are busy people. They want tactical advice, not just theory.

Should you always hit your sweet spot?

No, absolutely not. The sweet spot is a guideline, not a prison. Some topics naturally need more time — think a full course breakdown versus a quick tip. Others need less, like a news reaction compared to a deep dive.

The real takeaway here is awareness. If your sweet spot is, say, 10–15 minutes but you’re regularly posting 45-minute videos, that’s definitely worth investigating. Either your audience wants shorter content and you’re losing them, or your long videos are about different topics that attract a different (and likely smaller) audience.

The data should inform your decisions, not dictate them. If your 60-minute deep dive is the video you’re most proud of, you should absolutely publish it. Just know it’s likely to get fewer views than a tighter, 15-minute version of that same content. (That's just how it works.)
