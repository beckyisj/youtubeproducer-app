The Face Rule: Why 57% of Top Money Niche Thumbnails Use Close-Up Faces

March 3, 2026

👤I analyzed 776 YouTube thumbnails across 57 channels as part of my work for Ali Abdaal. The clearest differentiator between a high-performing thumbnail and an average one? How much of the frame the face actually fills.

Analysis as of March 2026 · Based on 776 long-form YouTube videos across 57 channels.

We all know thumbnails need a face. That's baseline advice now — 90% of thumbnails in competitive niches already have one. But "having a face" is often where the thought process stops.

The real question isn't if you use a face. It's how close that face is. My data suggests many creators are missing out on clicks by not cropping in tight enough.

What percentage of YouTube thumbnails use faces?

Out of 776 long-form videos across 57 channels, 90% included at least one face. But those numbers shifted a lot depending on the niche:

Money niche
98%
Creator niche
91%
Self-Improvement
87%
Lifestyle/Vibes
70%

Looking at the top 20 most-viewed videos in the whole dataset, 19 of them featured a face. The single exception was a cinematic lifestyle video. If your content is about education, finance, or advice, faces aren't just a good idea — they're non-negotiable.

Why close-up faces outperform mid-shots and wide shots

This is where things get interesting. "Face in thumbnail" is just the starting point. The real differentiator for top performers is proximity — how much of the frame that face actually fills.

Across the board, 49% of face thumbnails used a close-up shot (meaning forehead to chin, filling 40%+ of the frame). But in the Money niche — which, by the way, averages the highest views at 885K per video — that number jumps to 57%. That's a pretty significant difference.

So, why does proximity make such a big difference? I've noticed three key reasons:

  1. Expression readability. Thumbnails are tiny. On mobile, they're basically postage stamps. A mid-shot expression just gets lost at that size. A tight close-up, though, screams its emotion before anyone even registers the title.
  2. Pattern interruption. A big face in the feed instantly breaks the scroll. It's much harder to ignore a pair of eyes looking right at you than a full-body shot of someone standing in a studio.
  3. Emotional signal speed. Viewers make a click decision in under a second. A close-up face delivers an emotion instantly — curiosity, confidence, surprise, concern — before the viewer's conscious brain has even processed the words.

The 13% vs 57% gap: a case study

When I benchmarked one major creator (6M+ subscribers) against their Money niche peers, the difference in close-up usage was the most striking finding in the whole analysis.

This creator only used close-up face shots in 13% of their thumbnails. The niche average? 57%. That's a 44 percentage point gap — the biggest difference I measured for any single attribute.

Their typical thumbnail showed a mid-shot or wide shot, often looking away from the camera, with a neutral or thoughtful expression. Their competitors, meanwhile, filled the frame with confident, direct-eye-contact close-ups.

This creator still pulls in 1.4M views per video, so the content itself is clearly strong. But the packaging isn't quite aligned with what that niche responds to. The thumbnails signal "thoughtful professor" when the audience is looking for "confident authority."

Does eye contact make a difference in YouTube thumbnails?

Close-up faces really hit different when they're paired with direct eye contact. Here's what the data showed:

  • 77% of all face thumbnails had direct camera eye contact.
  • In the Money niche, that was 79%.
  • Among the top 20 most-viewed videos, it was 80%.

Eye contact triggers the same social brain circuits, whether you're face-to-face or looking at a 320x180 pixel thumbnail. It creates that instant "this person is talking to me" moment that looking off-camera just can't replicate.

The exceptions I've seen are tutorial-style thumbnails where the creator is looking at a screen, a whiteboard, or a product they're reviewing. These can absolutely work when the object itself is the main draw (think a new iPhone or a complex stock chart). But for talking-head content, opinion videos, and story-driven formats? Look right at the camera.

What expression should you use?

The ideal expression really depends on your niche, but the data does show some clear patterns:

Money niche: Serious (24%) and confident (17%) are the clear winners here. The expression basically says, "I've figured something out, and I'm about to explain it." Smiling is less common (24% total) — financial content often feels more credible with a serious face.

Creator niche: Smiling is the top expression. Creator content leans more into community, and a warm, inviting expression really matches that vibe.

High-energy expressions (excited, surprised, shocked) show up in just 13% of thumbnails, but they're in 15% of the top 20 most-viewed videos. They're a high-risk, high-reward move, for sure — overuse them and you'll just look clickbaity. But the occasional surprise face can seriously boost your CTR.

The one expression to avoid: neutral. It's common enough at 11%, but a neutral face communicates absolutely nothing. It's the visual equivalent of a totally generic title. Every thumbnail is prime billboard space — it needs to make the viewer feel something before they click.

How to apply the face rule to your thumbnails

So, here's a practical checklist based on what I've seen in the data:

  1. Crop from forehead to chin. Your face should take up at least 40% of the thumbnail. If I can see your waist, you're too far out.
  2. Look directly into the camera lens. No slight glances away, no looking at a product unless it's the actual subject. Straight into the lens. About 77–80% of top thumbnails do this.
  3. Match your expression to the video's promise. Teaching about money? Go for serious or confident. Sharing a story? Try surprised or concerned. Reviewing something fun? Smile. But always go all-in — a half-hearted expression is worse than none at all.
  4. Test it at thumbnail size. Shrink your thumbnail down to the size it'll actually appear in the feed. Can you still clearly read the expression? If not, crop tighter or exaggerate it more.
  5. Use a dark background to make the face pop. About 38–41% of top thumbnails use dark or moody schemes. That contrast makes the face the undeniable focal point.

Audit your own thumbnails

The quickest way to spot your own opportunities is to put your last 10 thumbnails side-by-side. Count how many use close-ups, direct eye contact, and a really clear expression. If your numbers are below the benchmarks I just mentioned, that's your easiest win for improving CTR.
